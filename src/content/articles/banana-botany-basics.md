## The banana "tree" is not a tree

The banana plant is the world's largest **herbaceous flowering plant** — botanically an herb. Its "trunk" is actually a **pseudostem**: tightly rolled leaf bases with no wood at all. The true stem grows underground (a corm), and after fruiting once, the pseudostem dies and a new shoot ("pup") takes its place.

## The banana is a berry (the strawberry isn't)

In botanical terms, a berry is a fleshy fruit produced from a single flower's ovary, with seeds embedded inside. Bananas qualify. Strawberries — whose "seeds" are on the outside — do not. Feel free to ruin fruit salad conversations with this forever.

## Why bananas are curved

Bananas exhibit **negative geotropism**: the fruit starts growing downward, then turns and grows upward toward the light as the bunch hangs. The result is the familiar curve.

## Clones, all the way down

Commercial bananas are **seedless and sterile**. Those tiny dark specks in the center are the vestiges of seeds that never develop. Wild bananas, by contrast, are packed with hard, pea-sized seeds and barely any flesh. Because cultivated bananas can't reproduce sexually, every new plant is grown from cuttings or tissue culture — meaning nearly every Cavendish banana on Earth is a **genetic clone** of the same plant.

That uniformity is why your banana always tastes the same — and, as the Gros Michel learned the hard way, why a single disease can threaten the entire world supply at once.

## Bunch vocabulary

A full stem of bananas is called a **bunch**; each tier on the stem is a **hand**; a single banana is a **finger**. Yes, really — and the word "banana" itself likely traces to the Arabic *banan*, meaning fingertips.
