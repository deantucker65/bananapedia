Until the 1950s, the banana in every American and European fruit bowl was the **Gros Michel** ("Big Mike"). It was creamier, sweeter, and sturdier than today's banana — it shipped so well that whole stems could be tossed into ship holds without padding.

## The fungus that changed everything

Starting in the early 1900s, a soil fungus causing **Panama disease** (Fusarium wilt) began spreading through Central American plantations. Because commercial bananas are clones — every plant genetically identical — the disease tore through them like wildfire. There was no resistant Gros Michel to breed from and no fungicide that worked.

Growers literally moved the industry across Central America, abandoning infected land and cutting new plantations out of the jungle, but the fungus always followed. By the **early 1960s**, the Gros Michel was commercially dead in the Americas.

## Enter the Cavendish

The industry's savior was the **Cavendish** — a banana that had been kept as a curiosity in the greenhouse of England's Chatsworth House, home of the Dukes of Devonshire (family name: Cavendish). It resisted Panama disease but was blander and more fragile, forcing the invention of the modern banana box.

## The candy conspiracy that isn't (quite)

A favorite banana fact: artificial banana flavoring tastes "wrong" because it was based on the Gros Michel. The truth is more nuanced — the flavor compound (isoamyl acetate) was created before flavorists targeted any specific banana — but the Gros Michel genuinely does contain more of that compound. So candy bananas really do taste more like the banana of 1920 than the one in your kitchen.

The Gros Michel isn't extinct — it survives in small plantings in parts of Asia, the Pacific, and the Caribbean. If you ever get the chance to try one, take it.
